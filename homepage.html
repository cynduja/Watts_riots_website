1965

On a hot summer evening in Watts, Marquette Frye, an African-American man, was pulled over by a white California Highway Patrol officer, who suspected Frye of driving under the influence of alcohol.

A crowd gathered and tension between police officers and people quickly escalated into a violent outburst. For the next six days, violence and mayhem led to the loss of 34 lives, 1032 injuries, 3438 arrests, and over $40 million in property damage.

Watts Rebellion Revisited

The incident, called the "Watts rebellion" by many, was one of the largest urban revolutions of the civil rights era.

Martin Luther King told reporters that the riots were, "the beginning of a stirring of those people in our society who have been bypassed by the progress of the past decade." This project aims to examine the events leading up to the rebellion and its aftermath using visual web techniques for the upcoming 50th anniversary and explore what has changed in the community since then.

Aftermath

During the six days of riots, 14,000 California National Guard troops were sent in to restore peace. Curfew zones spanning 45 miles were established. An official investigation conducted by Governor Pat Brown concluded that the riots were an expression of grievances resulting from a poor quality of life.

The commission recommended "emergency literacy programs," "establishing a job training and placement center," and several other measures to improve the quality of education and create more employment opportunities.

Source of text: Violence in the city: An end or a beginning?, the Governor's report on the riots

Many of these recommendations were not implemented by the authorities.
